Project Overview

Current technology trends and travel methods suggest that the travel experience is some singular assignment for would-be travelers, and, little contrivances appears to be give advantage to a growing body of tech savvy voyagers that require efficient options and pseudo-AI to complete their tasks. Swift capitalizes on this important need by allowing users to quickly plot out vacation plans, which include the most critical part of the preparations: what to do and how much its going to cost. This study will show how a simple focus on the itinerary aspect of travel can improve economies of scale.

The Problem

Prospective travelers feel hopeless about committing to trips. Only a sliver of trips escape from the chamber of communication and online checkout points to become real. They need to create efficiently tailored travel plans, which are inclusive of the costs of activities, but they face irrelevant online advice and the absence of a system to design plans based on how they feel, because ultimately, they don't know what to do.

The Goal

To fill the space in curating a schedule for a upcoming/potential vacation(s). Delivering a primary problem solving tool in this key area of preparation, which would drastically change the abandonment rate of would-be travelers. Ultimately, to diminish the number of times travel plans become nothing greater than friendly discussion fodder and cart dreams.

In order for the design to maximize core UX principles I determined that this part of the research process should involve A/B testing, as well as, 5-second impression test, especially, of the most critical frames -- the dashboard and main feature. 1) Persons that reflected the demographic uncovered during phase 1 -- 'User Research' -- were involved.
2) Test data was interpreted by filtering through a numerical value order (number of votes for) and a 2 round system

Usability Testing

The process used during usability testing was reconstructive. Respondents were provided several scenarios of a hypothetical user's need(s) then were asked to use the app to satisfy the need(s). The respondent were asked to advise the tester after each step was completed, and, describe the level of difficulty involved in each scenario. Data was interpreted quantitatively, and as such, total success/failure values of each respondent was computed by a tier system given to groups of percentiles in each scenario. By understanding the number and type(s) of failures in particular frame(s) of a flow or given task I was able to focus on changes to buttons, asset placement, and sometimes text/content change.

Key Insights

Additional navigation buttons at the top of the frame such as the home button were unnecessary and could be added to the bottom nav.

A "Save" button or option should be added to affirm the users plan progress. As well, this button was also part of a familiar experience with competitors and like apps.

Iconography and field layout in initial lo-fi and mid-di iterations gave users the impression they could make changes on those frames. They were removed in final design to ensure success.

'Sliders' as preference setter were preferred where applicable as they were a quicker form of establishing information, and, helped users progress faster.

Style Guide

User research was an important basis for style guide choices. The colors and typography were meant to capture the thrill, tranquility, and purposefulness of travel -- themes acknowledged by users. Iconography, as well, were intended to be a playful yet solid. All of these items are to be softer as to align with the sentiments expressed during research, and ultimately, deliver greater ‘enjoyability’.

Conclusions

Lessons Learned

Surprisingly, the most important lesson(s) came, not during the later testing portion of design, but rather during the user research phase. I began this study with the sense that I would blend a feature I had been considering for sometime with what data came out of my user research (yes, admittedly, I started this project with a lot of solution related prejudice). User research was especially enlightening. It provided me with the tagline for Swift, and, most importantly, the central component of Swift -- package based mood selection. Inquiry allowed me to narrow in on the issues with redundancy in CTAs and buttons, which, until streamlined, were adding onto the time it took for users to complete tasks. Ultimately, preliminary research is what generated all of the algorithmic, and dare I say, most satisfying parts of Swift.

  • The current model of traveler psychology is far less well explored than travel products would suggest. Many products are aimed at giving travelers many options for air line, hotel, and car selection/booking, but not for creating a daily itinerary. It would seem most travel companies/products have abandoned this large plurality of travelers, or, worse, are unaware they even exist.

  • Fields and containers of user data should not be used when information of user inputs are merely being communicated to the user as this creates confusion, hampers user task progression and could lead to app abandonment.

Next Steps

With the lessons learned during guerilla testing it behooves me to further develop other important feature related aspects of Swift that develop the gamification of it such as, 'avatar achievements' and the Play-list specific map(s). These features completed the user experience of Swift, but were not able to be developed and deployed within the timeframe of this project.
